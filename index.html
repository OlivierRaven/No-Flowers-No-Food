The biodiversity crisis ‚Äî why we must act now

Global assessments show nature is in rapid decline. The IPBES Global Assessment (2019) warns that up to one million species risk extinction. The WWF Living Planet Report 2024 finds an average 73% decline in wildlife populations since 1970. These trends threaten food security, clean water, climate stability, and our quality of life.

In Aotearoa, our biodiversity is unique and vulnerable. Many species are found nowhere else. Pollution, habitat loss, invasive species, and disease all play a role. See the Department of Conservation on pests and threats.

Nature supports everything

Pollination, soil building, nutrient cycling, pest control, and water purification are services that healthy ecosystems provide to people every day.

We are losing it fast

Habitat fragmentation, overuse of chemicals, climate change, and invasive species are accelerating declines. Without action, losses become harder to reverse.

Good news

Nature rebounds quickly when we give it space. Simple changes in how we manage lawns and public spaces can create corridors for insects and birds within months.

Why insects matter for food, people, and place

Pollination = food

Many crops in NZ rely on pollinators: kiwifruit, apples, stone fruit, seed crops, and more. Honeybees help, but bumblebees, native bees, hoverflies, moths, butterflies, and even some birds contribute. See Manaaki Whenua: Pollination & pollinators.

Native uniqueness

New Zealand has ~22,000 described arthropod species and many more yet to be identified. About 80% are endemic. Protecting insects protects what makes Aotearoa special. See Manaaki Whenua on invertebrate diversity.

Beyond bees

Night-flying moths, flies, and birds also pollinate, including in cooler and alpine places. Research finds moths can act as pollinators even in Subantarctic NZ. See Polar Research (2019).

Insects connect ecosystems

Insects feed birds and fish, recycle nutrients, and keep soils alive. Lose insects and everything else suffers. Recovery starts with flowers, nesting sites, and chemical-free spaces.

Do this today ‚Äî simple actions with big impact

Nature does not only live in reserves. It lives everywhere. Your lawn, verge, balcony, and school grounds can be part of the solution.

Don‚Äôt mow in peak flowering

Pause mowing in spring and summer so flowers can bloom and feed pollinators. Inspired by No Mow May and related campaigns.

Don‚Äôt spray

Avoid herbicides and pesticides. Manage weeds by hand, mulch, or design with native groundcovers. Chemical-free spaces bring insects back quickly.

Plant native wildflowers and shrubs

Choose varied flowering times to support pollinators year-round. Include nesting habitat like bare patches for ground-nesting bees and tussocks.

Let edges grow

Leave parts of lawns, road edges, and berms to grow naturally. Connect patches into a local corridor so insects can move through neighbourhoods.

Support pollinator-friendly farms & councils

Encourage Bee Friendly Farming practices and local bylaws that reduce mowing and spraying. See Bee Friendly Farming.

Join & share

Volunteer with local groups and share your wild patches on social media. Community action normalises nature-inclusive streets and schools.

The benefits of being nature-inclusive

Food security

Pollinators keep orchards, gardens, and farms productive. Without flowers there is no food. Fewer chemicals also protect beneficial insects and soils.

Health and wellbeing

Green streets reduce stress, improve mood, and invite active living. See Wageningen: Nature & health in the city.

Climate resilience

Wildflower verges and green roofs cool cities and help soak up stormwater. Nature-inclusive design reduces heat and flood risks. See Sempergreen.

Beauty and place

Colourful meadows, birdsong, and butterflies make neighbourhoods more welcoming and distinct. Small patches add up.

Community connection

Garden strips, school meadows, and shared planters bring people together and create pride in local streets.

Costs down over time

Less mowing and spraying can cut maintenance costs when areas are designed well and allowed to naturalise.

FAQ ‚Äî common questions

Will long grass look messy?

Design makes all the difference. Frame wild areas with a mown edge or path, add a small sign, and mix in flowering natives. People read it as intentional.

Are ‚Äúweeds‚Äù like clover and dandelions really OK?

Yes. They are valuable nectar sources in early spring when food is scarce. Replace spraying with selective hand weeding and let flowers feed insects.

What about allergies or safety?

Choose lower‚Äëallergen species and keep sightlines open around driveways. In public spaces, add simple signage and maintain clear edges for accessibility.

What if my council has mowing schedules?

Ask for a pilot. Point to No Mow May and EU Pollinator City guides. Propose ‚Äúmosaic mowing‚Äù, reduced spray zones, and seasonal flowering windows.
